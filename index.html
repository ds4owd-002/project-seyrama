Introduction

Project description

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the importance of water, sanitation, hygiene, and infection prevention and control (WASH/IPC) infrastructure in health care facilities, particularly in resource-constrained settings. In Ghana, primary health care facilities serve as the first point of contact for most patients and play a critical role in preventing the spread of infectious diseases during outbreaks. As part of a project to improve WASH access in the Upper East Region, a facility-level assessment was conducted in May 2022 across 39 primary health care facilities in four municipalities. The assessment generated data on water supply, sanitation, hygiene facilities, and health care waste management to inform pandemic preparedness planning.

Methods

I used data collected in May 2022 from 39 primary health care facilities across four municipalities in Ghana’s Upper East Region. Trained field teams collected the data using structured questionnaires administered through the mWater mobile data collection platform, combining direct observation with facility-reported information on water, sanitation, hygiene, and health care waste management.

Water, Sanitation and Hygiene availability

An indicator of basic WASH access, defined as the simultaneous availability of water, usable toilets, and functioning hygiene stations was generated. Facilities were classified as having basic WASH access only when all three conditions were met.

82% of facilities had access to basic water, sanitation and hygiene as shown in Table 1. Although 82% of facilities met the criteria for basic WASH, this estimate applies only to facilities with existing sanitation infrastructure and complete WASH data. A separate assessment of toilet availability as shown in ?@fig-functioning-toilets below shows that many facilities lacked any toilet or latrine.

Functionality of sanitation and hygiene infrastructure

?@fig-functioning-toilets illustrates the availability and functionality of toilets across facilities. Many facilities lacked any toilet or latrine, while fewer had non-functioning toilets. Among facilities with toilet infrastructure, functioning toilets were more common than non-functioning ones, indicating that the primary sanitation gap is the absence of toilets rather than functionality.
